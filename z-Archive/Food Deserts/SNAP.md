Policy initiatives for improving the healthfulness of food choice range from government mandates to information provision, but economic incentives remain a popular choice (Gorski and Roberto 2015). The Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program[[1]](#_ftn1) (SNAP) is a well-studied example of an economic incentive for alleviating food insecurity. The methodology applied to determine the effects of SNAP on health outcomes can be instructive for understanding consumption preferences.  SNAP is the largest food assistance program in the United States. It is administered at the state level, but the federal government provides funding. SNAP uptake is less than perfect and suffers from endogenous selection, making identifying its impact on nutrition and health difficulties. Evidence shows that SNAP recipients are more economically disadvantaged and less likely to be in excellent or very good health (Bitler 2014). This issue of endogeneity is one of the largest problems in identifying the effects of economic incentives on food insecurity.
