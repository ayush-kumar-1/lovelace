In his 1982 paper, Victor Fuchs puts forth what is now known as the Fuchs Hypothesis. By this time, the relationship between schooling and health had been established by both objective measures (life expectancy, BMI, etc.) and by subjective measures (self-perceived health status). Economist Michael Grossman dubbed this the efficient producer hypothesis/effect. Those with higher levels of education were more efficient producers of health. This could be because of healthcare-seeking tendencies, but Fuchs offers a different mechanism: time preference. Accepting current costs, i.e., displeasure from eating broccoli, pain from running, or financial cost from visiting the doctors for a future health benefit, is a form of health investment. Like other forms of investment, a person will choose to invest in their health if their returns over the investment horizon are greater than their utility from the opportunity cost (unhealthy activities). Future value is derived from an individual's discount rate, $\rho$, or what is known as their time preference. Take a simple investment with a return $r$ as an example. Let $u_0$ represent the utility that can be derived from the initial investment. 

$$\rho = 1- r$$
$$NPV = \sum_{t=1}^\infty \rho^t u_t - u_0$$
An individual will invest if the net present value (NPV) of the investment is greater than 0, or put in simpler terms, if the investment will pay off. Fuchs extends this concept into health investment. A person will engage in a health-enchancing activity if they believe that their investment will pay off. Fuchs hypothesizes that individuals will have different time preferences (differing discount rates). Individuals with discount rates closer to one will be more likely to delay instant gratification for more investmetn in both education and health. The relationship between higher income and more schooling is also well established. Higher socioeconomic status may also be correlated with lower discount rates through other causal mechanisms. 

In the context of food deserts, this time preference would manifest itself as a demand problem. The USDA defines a food desert as an area with both low-income and low-access to food. If food deserts are a demand problem it may be that residents of food deserts have lower discount rates, and simply demand less healthy food. This interpretation means that time preference is the underlying causal mechanism for food deserts, fewer years of education, and income. 

This conclusion is well supported by the literature, but an important follow-up question emerges: how is individual time preference set? Fuchs posits that time preference is established early in life and may be a combination of genetics, family income, religous beliefs, and other environmental factors. If this is true, then food deserts exist becuase there is portion of the American population with low discount rates who lead unhealthier lives, go to less school, and make less money that just so happen to conglomerate in food deserts. This story of food deserts paints the demand problem is an individual moral failure. Furthermore we find food deserts have higher proportions of black and hispanic populations. Chalking up these differences to genetics would fundamentally go against critical race theory and quickly takes a turn for phrenology. 

The answer then is in large part structural and environmental. This may be linked to cycles of poverty and deeper historical throughlines. This interpretation of Fuchs hypothesis is the one I find most feasible, maybe as a result of nothing more than personal beliefs. 

Here I lay out a theory of what is happening in food deserts. For my first assumption I borrow from Fuchs with modification: time preference is established early in life and largely through enviornmental factors and upbringing. Time preference is a part of the underlying reason for lack of demand when it comes to food deserts. Therefore, educational and informational initiatives to improve demand should focus on changing the internal time preference of individuals living in food deserts.
