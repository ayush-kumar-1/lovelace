**Food Deserts**: Food deserts are geographic areas where residents have few to no convenient options for securing affordable and healthy foods, especially fresh fruits and vegetables. Disproportionately found in high-poverty areas, food deserts create extra everyday hurdles that can make it harder for kids, families, and communities to grow healthy and strong. ([aecf.org](http://aecf.org/))

**Food Insecurity**: Household-level economic and social conditions of limited or uncertain access to adequate food. (Food Insecurity Roundtable)

Uncertainty in the causal mechanism for socioeconomic disparities in health (SDH), many are inclined to ask:  [[Do Food Deserts Exist?]]

Food deserts and food insecurity are woven together to tell a certain story. Low-access households are characterized as being far away from healthy food options and low income. Populations that live within low-access census tracts are more likely to belong to black and Hispanic minority groups. These low-access groups have higher rates of diabetes, depression, obesity, and lower life expectancy. It is difficult to establish the role that food deserts play (if any) in creating and perpetuating socioeconomic disparities in health. 

## What is Healthy Food? 
Nutrition is complicated. The effects of nutrition on the human body are even more complicated. For example, fruits and vegetables are generally considered healthy, but many proteins, grains, and fats must accompany them. Furthermore, nutrition is a scale. Donuts are unhealthier than white bread, which is unhealthier than multi-grain bread. Still, donuts are healthier than pure sugar. There is also the matter of caloric intake, which is the root cause of obesity. A caloric deficit might be healthy for some individuals, whereas others may need a surplus. 

## Supply and Demand 
In the journal of policy retrospectives, Marianne Bitler and Steven Haider wrote "An Economic View of Food Deserts in the United States." Efficient markets find the right combination of supply and demand to determine "what products are available, where they are available, and at what price they are available" (Bitler & Haider 2011).

If food deserts are a result of an insufficient supply of fresh and healthy foods in communities that want them, it represents a market failure. On the other hand, if demand for healthy foods is lacking in low-access areas, then the market will correctly avoid opening fresh grocers and farmers' markets in the area. This points to healthy food as a normal good being the reason for the low supply. According to this hypothesis, low-income areas demand healthy food less than high-income areas. The issue of demand may come down to the cost of healthy food. Bitler & Haider warn against "simply allowing for differences in tastes for healthy food by race and ethnicity or educational level because it has the potential for 'assuming' away the problem." In other words, assuming that poor and minority communities don't like healthy food means food deserts are the markets working efficiently.

As mentioned above, one hypothesis for these differences in taste comes down to cost. In this case, economic incentives for purchasing healthier food would then increase the outcomes of these communities. Another hypothesis is that individuals with lower socioeconomic status (SES) individuals have larger discount rates. This leads to less investment in health and education (Fuchs 1982, [[Fuchs Hypothesis]]). Some other hypotheses for lower demand in food deserts include: 
- Time costs of obtaining ingredients and cooking. Deferring to pre-cooked and fast-food meals which in general have worse nutrition. This effect becomes more pronounced in families with children. 
- Inadequate information about the relative merits of different food choices. People may not be educated enough to make the right decisions for their health. 
- Other factors that influence demand include advertising and information provided about products. 
- Behavioral factors that apply to food. This may include self-control, time-inconsistent preferences, and the importance of habits. 

Supply issues may include high fixed costs and small store sizes. Bitler and Haider point out three potential barriers to supply: 
1. Economies of Scale - larger stores are more profitable than smaller ones 
2. Economies of Scope - less variety makes the store less profitable 
3. Economies of agglomeration - per unit costs decline when more stores are nearby, less stores means higher costs for opening and operating

**Market Power**
Market power may be uncertain in food deserts. While the retail offering of food would intuitively into the perfect or monopolistic competition, food deserts are areas without much competition, which may create oligopolies or monopolies. 

Notions of perfect competition and market efficiency assume only a narrow definition of economic efficiency. It may be that policymakers and the public have greater concerns, including equity and health outcomes.
