# Introduction to Food Insecurity 

The United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) Economic Research Service (ERS) defines food security as access at all times to enough food for an active and healthy life[^1]. 

Gucciardi et al. find that food-insecure households have a higher prevalence and increased risk of diabetes even after controlling for risk factors like income, employment status, physical measures, and lifestyle factors. 

Bravemen et al. describe socioeconomic disparities in health in the United States. The relationship between education, income, and health outcomes is well established [^2]. Blacks and Hispanics have lower educational attainment and higher poverty rates than Whites. 

Peterson et al. use Center for Disease Control (CDC) data to quantity racial and ethnic disparities in adult obesity. The obesity epidemic often begins with early feeding patterns in children strongly influenced by their parents. Obesity is defined as a BMI of 30 or higher. Non-Hispanic black adults experience the highest rates of obesity by any racial category. Hispanic adults follow them closely. The causes of these differences reflect social and economic advantages correlated to race. The CDC's Division of Nutrition, Physical Activity, and Obesity (DNPAO) cites the need to access and procure healthier foods to lower obesity.
