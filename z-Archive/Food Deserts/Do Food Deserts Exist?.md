Of course, areas of low access do exist, but this question challenged the accepted wisdom that increasing access will improve health outcomes for people living in these areas. Dr. Chen Zhen of the Department of Agricultural and Applied Economics here at UGA writes about previous efforts in economics to establish causal relationships between food deserts and adverse health outcomes. His review of the literature concludes, "All things considered, there is so far little evidence that food deserts have a causal effect of meaningful magnitude on health and nutritional disparities" (Zhen 2021). Zhen argues that most of the health disparities come from the demand side, not the supply side. The following table summarizes the causal studies evaluated in his review.
