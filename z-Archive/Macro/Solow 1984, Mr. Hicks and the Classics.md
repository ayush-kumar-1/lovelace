In "Mr. Hicks and the Classics," economist Robert Solow creates two characters. Sir John and J.R. to entertain attacks and offer defenses of the IS-LM apparatus. J.R. is the familiar face who wrote "Mr. Keynes and the Classics" ([[Hicks 1937, Mr. Keynes and the "Classics" A Suggested Interpretation]]). Solow's fundamental claim is that "IS-LM survived because it proved to be a marvelously simple and useful way to organize and process some of the main macroeconomic facts. 

Sir John represents an older John Hicks, one who was "not entirely hostile to it. But he did suggest that the original construction had some pretty fundamental problems to get over." The other critic considered is Axel Leijonhufvud, a man staunchly against the IS-LM apparatus. 

## Prices and Wages
JR assumes that IS-LM refers to a unit period within which the nominal wage could be taken as fixed. This is because the nominal output could be used as an index for real output and employment. Unfortunately, this does not hold true because the real output is not necessarily proportionally to the nominal output. 

The common presumption has been "that the price level should or could be taken equal to the marginal cost of output, and therefore rising as output and employment rise." In this model, inflation is never a bad thing because price levels are always increasing due to an increase in real output. Unfortunately, this is not true. This assumption forces the LM curve to be flatter at low levels of output, and to be very steep at high levels of output, representing the steepness of the marginal cost of output. 

These assumptions and results lead Solow to the prophetic conclusion that "When the macroeconomic problem is dominated by partial adjustment to inflation and its aggregative and distributional consequences, IS-LM is not the right model or is only a part of the right model."

Solow now references "The Classics Again", written by Hicks, or as Solow dubs him now -- Professor H. The defense offered is simple, IS-LM depends heavily on small moves within the given time periods. It simply will not do in periods of heavy inflation. Solow "doubts that it violates common observation to suggest that, in fact, wage rates do not respond very flexibly to economic events on a time-scale less than a year." Another argument offered is that "there is ample evidence that real wages have no strong endogenous pattern in modern industrial economies." 

Solow asks that "we practice thinking of the IS curve, not as a locus along which 'the goods market clears' but as a locus along which 'the wage-employment bargain is an equilibrium given a goods-market outcome that is an equilibrium given the wage-employment bargain'."

## Expectations 

The IS-LM apparatus has little to say on the matter of expectations. Solow views a minor problem and a major problem arising from this. The minor problem -- the investment component of effective demand. Of course, investment decisions are based on discounting rates and forward-looking decision-making. Unfortunately, as a static model, "this may be the best we can do." Solow offers adding a separate variable known as expected output and computing effective demand as a function of expected output, current output, and interest rate. The existing stock of capital goods would also have to be in there, and this creates a certain "form of the capital-stock adjustment principle." To make these expectations endogenous is difficult, and Solow calls upon an "Icarus waiting to try" to create a model that can incorporate them. In short, the IS curves (more particular the I component) can be adjusted to account for expectations partially through existing variables, and partially through exogenous intervention. 

The more pressing issue, Solow's major problem, enters through the LM curve. IS-LM is a flow model, but the LM is curve is defined as the set of points that clears the money market, $M_s = M_d$. Here lies the rub, money supply is a stock variable. Thus, the LM curve represents a stock relation and throughout the period there must exist a stock equilibrium. The idea of money demanded means that there is speculation in the market, and thereby expectations going unfulfilled. Hicks settled the issue by allowing for stochastic liquidity preference, but Solow points out three issues with this approach 
1. The inherent difficulty in integrating stocks and flows. 
2. The difficulty of of fitting money into equilibrium models 
3. Probabilistic equilibria do not make a sufficient concession to true uncertainty, i.e. the model functions as usual without really considering the logistics of what's happening. 
Solow claims that "none of these difficulties is peculiar to IS-LM." The problem of stocks and flows is a more general one and not a pure indictment of the model. 

## Stocks and Flows
IS-LM may be suitable for long-run analysis based on the Binder-Solow device, but the short-run issues between stocks and flows remains. Fundamentally the modeling problem is seen as substantive, not philosophical, and many frameworks in the future have been built on top of the IS-LM.
