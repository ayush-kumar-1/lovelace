

Hicks's goal is to compare a classical theory of money with a Keynesian interpretation. Hicks assumes a short-run horizon with homogenous labor. Depreciation is assumed to be negligible and the output of investment goods corresponds to new investment. 
- $w$ is the fixed rate of wages 
- $x$, $y$ are the outputs of investment goods and consumption goods respectively 
- $N_x, N_y$ are the number of men employed in producing those goods
- $x = f_x(N_x), y = f_y(N_y)$ where the functions are givens
- $M$ is the given quantity of money
The price of investment goods will be their marginal cost 
$$MC_x = w \frac{dN_x}{dx}$$
Income earned in investment trades, or value of investment is represented as 
$$I_x=wx\frac{dN_x}{d_y} $$
Income earned as consumption trades will be 
$$I_y=wy\frac{dN_x}{d_x}$$
Total income $I$ is written as 
$$I = I_x + I_y$$
Hicks assumes the "Cambridge Quantity equation" that there is some definite relation between Income and the demand for money. Demand for money may not depend only upon total Income, but also upon its distribution between people 
$$M = kI$$
In order to determine $I_x$ two equations are needed the first is the amount of investment or demand for capital. 
$$I_x = C(i)$$
This is what becomes the marginal-efficiency-of-capital schedule. Thus we arrive at three fundamental equations 
$$M = kI, I_x=C(i), I_x=S(i, I)$$
Little $i$ is the rate of interest. Given this model Hick's considers some properties of this system. 
1. As soon as $k$ and $M$ are given, $I$ is completely determined. total income depends directly on the quantity of money. However total employment is not necessarily determined at once from income, since it will usually depend upon the proportion of income saved, and thus upon the way production is divided between investment and consumption goods.
2. An increase in the inducement to invest (i.e., a rightward movement of $C(i)$) will tend to raise the rate of interest, and affect saving. If the amount of saving rises, the amount of investment will too; labour will be employed more in the investment trades, less in the consumption trades. 
3. An increase in the supply of money will raise total income; people will increase their spending and lending until incomes have risen sufficiently to restore k to its former level. The rise in income will tend to increase employment, both in making consumption goods and in making investment goods. 
4. A rise in the rate of money wages will necessarily diminish employment and raise real wages. 

## Keynesian Modeling
Here, Hick's points out some limitations of the classical model. Chiefly that classicist's assume that $k$ and $M$ are the only way to account for variations within a cycle. $M$ changes much, but there are not easy answers to how $M$ varies within a business cycle. $k$ may not be an independent variable because the amount of saving may vary with the economic environment, optimism, or other exogenous factors. Thus, we begin with the Keynesian model 
$$M = L(i), I_x = C(i), I_x = S(I)$$
There are two primary ways in which the Keynesian model varies from the classical model 
1. The demand for money is conceived as depending upon the rate of interest (*Liquidity Preference*)
2. Any possible influence on the rate of interest on the amount saved out of a given income is neglected 
It is the rate of interest, not income, which is determined by the quantity of money. Therefore the volume of employment is given by the value of investment and of income which is not saved but spent upon consumption of goods. Therefore an increase in the inducement to invest, or in the propensity to consume, will not tend to raise the rate of interest, but only to increase employment. We modify this to include $L$ as function of income as well because transactions are critical as well. 
$$M = L(I, i), I_x = C(i), I_x = S(I)$$
Hicks reconciles the differences between the different schools of thought in curve that shows that a given supply a money determines a certain relation between income and interest. He calls this the LL curve (LM in class). There will be a portion that tends to be nearly horizontal on the left, nearly vertical on the right, and a mid-point region as well. 
1. At the upward-sloping section of the curve the classical theory will be a good approximation. An increase in the inducement to invest will raise the rate of interest but it will have some subsidiary effect in raising income, and therefore employment as well. 
2. At the nearly horizontal (or left side) of the curve then the special form Keynesian theory applies. A rise in the schedule of the marginal efficiency of capital will only increases employment and does not effect the rate of interest at all. Here "we are completely out of touch with the classical world." 
Where we are on the LM curve is important. If IS lies to the right then we can indeed increase employment by increasing the quantity of money. If IS lies to the left we cannot do so. Monetary policy will be unable to force down the rate of interest any further. 
Hicks then posits a hybrid theory where $I, i$ are present in all equations. 
$$M = L(I,i), I_x=C(I,i), I_x=S(I,i)?$$
First, the Generalized General theory draw a curve CC showing the marginal efficiency of capital at a given Income. A curve SS shows the supply curve of saving at that given Income. The following conclusions come out of this model:
1. If there is "full employment" then it is possible that IS is horizontal. Then the investment rate becomes the natural rate. If there is a perfectly elastic monetary system, and the money rate is below the natural rate, there is cumulative inflation. 
2. If we have wide unemployment IS can be relied upon to be downward-sloping. Here is where Keynes' theories work the best. 
3. There may be situations where expectations are positive and create inflationary environments.
